Math Modeling

Description: This course goes beyond the traditional high school mathematics curriculum by engaging students in open-ended problem solving, computer simulations, and collaborative work. Students use a mathematical approach to model real-world situations through the application of algebra, geometry, trigonometry, and statistics.

This math course is unique to me because it is very interactive and geared toward student conversation rather than teacher lectures. Almost every class starts off with going over the homework with your tablemates and gets the ball rolling in a good way. The problems which are solved in class require outside of the box thinking and are similar to olympiad style questions. Currently, I am on the advanced mathematics track at MAMS and am taking Calculus III and IV at WPI. Some of my group projects are below.

HiMCM

The High School Mathematical Contest in Modeling (HiMCM) is an international contest designed to provide high school and middle school level students with the opportunity to work as team members to engage and improve their modeling, problem solving, and writing skills. Here at MAMS, we (Suhruth Vuppala, Kweku Akese, Jaylin Li, and I) had 72 hours to submit our solution. We stayed up late into the night and slept over for three days to complete the 2022 modeling problem. Although it was hard and tiring, it was really satisfying at the end to submit our solution. We chose to pick problem B because we felt that it contained more data to supplement our models to the solution of the problem. The general method of our solution was to regress the data points in various ways to get different graphs to predict future data.

Epsilon School

The Epsilon School problem was a mathematical modeling problem in which my group (Lily Pattison, Emily Wang, and I) had to hire new teachers to the Epsilon School because a new wing is being added. The student population will be increased from 490 to 630 for the next school year. In total, seven additional faculty will be hired. Given student enrollments for the past two years, should every major discipline (English, Social Studies, Mathematics, Physics, Biology, Chemistry, and Foreign Language) each receive one new teacher, or does the demand for courses argue that some departments should receive two new teachers while others receive none? My group decided to go with the latter.
