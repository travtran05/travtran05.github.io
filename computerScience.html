Agram

Agram is an American Computer Science (ACSL) programming problem of Senior Division Contest #1 in the 2016-2017 academic year. The problem is to develop a a program to play the game Agram. Agram is a card game for 2 players, using the cards from a standard 52-card pack. The dealer deals five cards to each player. The opponent player leads any card, playing it face up. The program will determine what card the dealer will play. Here are the rules of the game:

— The dealer must play a card of the same suit if they can.

— The dealer plays the lowest card in the suit that is of a higher rank than the card the opponent played.

— If the dealer does not have such a card, they play their lowest card in that suit.

— If the dealer does not have a card in that suit, they play the lowest ranking card regardless of suit.

— If there is a tie, they play the card in the following order: clubs, diamonds, hearts, and spades.

There will be 5 lines of input. Each line will contain the opponent’s lead card and the 5 cards held by the dealer. All cards will be represented by 2-character strings in value-suit order. AH represents the ace of hearts. K, Q and J and T will be used for king, queen, jack and 10 respectively. Note that the ace in this game has the lowest rank. For each input line, print the card the dealer must play.

ML Model for March Madness

One of the most unpredictable events in the modern era is the NCAA Division I Men's Basketball Tournament, commonly known as March Madness. With 68 teams competing and 67 games played, there are 2^67 possible bracket combinations. That is a number comparable to the number of grains of sand on the Earth. In addition, upsets are extremely common. All four number one seeds have made it to the Final Four only five times out of the 82 years the tournament has taken place. Suhruth Vuppala and I want to create a machine learning algorithm that can predict each match with a high probability, with the ultimate goal of getting every match prediction right.

Starting with sourced code from GitHub (which utilizes gradient boosted regression), we improved on the process by printing out a visualized version of the bracket instead of percentages to win. We only modified the model itself and not the preprocessing. In addition, we are currently working on our own ML model to try to predict the bracket better than the gradient boosted regression model we improved on.
